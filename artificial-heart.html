Project Context

Building an artificial heart.

The Turtle Robotics artificial heart project is a multidisciplinary student effort at Texas A&M aimed at designing and validating a functional artificial heart system. The project spans mechanical design, controls, and computational simulation — with teams working in parallel on each domain.

My contribution is on the simulation side, using ANSYS to model how blood flows through the current heart design. Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulation is a critical tool in cardiovascular device development — it allows engineers to identify flow anomalies, stagnation zones, and shear stress concentrations before any physical prototype is built.

Current Work

Learning ANSYS for cardiovascular CFD.

I'm currently building proficiency in ANSYS Fluent to simulate blood flow behavior within the artificial heart geometry. This involves setting up the fluid domain, defining boundary conditions that represent physiological blood flow (inlet velocity, pressure outlets, viscosity), and running steady-state simulations to visualize flow patterns.

Blood flow simulation is non-trivial — blood is a non-Newtonian fluid, meaning its viscosity changes with shear rate. Accurately modeling this behavior requires careful selection of viscosity models and mesh refinement in high-gradient regions.

Why this matters for structural heart: CFD simulation is used extensively in the development of TAVR devices, transcatheter heart valves, and ventricular assist devices. Building this skill as an undergraduate directly supports my goal of working in cardiovascular product development.

Trajectory

Where this is going.

This project is in its early stages — the simulation work is foundational right now, focused on building the technical competency to run meaningful analyses. As the heart design matures, the CFD work will shift toward validating specific design decisions: optimizing inlet geometry to reduce turbulence, identifying valve timing that minimizes recirculation, and characterizing wall shear stress distributions to predict thrombosis risk.

The goal is to have a simulation-validated design that can inform physical prototype development — the same workflow used in industry for cardiovascular device design verification.
