ANGEL — Soft Robotic
Fruit Harvesting Gripper

Institution Texas A&M Mechanical Engineering
Status Published
Role CAD Design, Prototyping, Experimental Testing
PublicationTPU 3D PrintingSolidWorks Cable-DrivenSoft RoboticsExperimental Validation

Co-authored peer-reviewed publication on a drawstring-inspired, cable-driven soft gripper for gentle fruit harvesting — achieving 0% immediate damage rate across 56 tomatoes with a lightweight, low-complexity design.

Overview

Gentle grasping without sensors.

Conventional robotic grippers damage soft fruits through concentrated pressure at rigid contact points. ANGEL takes a fundamentally different approach — inspired by a drawstring bag, two TPU-based pockets constrict around the fruit when cables are pulled, distributing force uniformly across the surface.

The result is a gripper that handles fruits of varying sizes and shapes with zero immediate damage and under 9% minor bruising after five days — outperforming machine-based harvesting (typically 20–29% damage rate) with fewer components and simpler control than competing designs.

Physical test — gripper opening and closing cycle

My Contributions

Spool design, pockets & testing.

My work focused on three areas: CAD design of structural components, TPU pocket iteration, and experimental trial execution.

For the CAD work, I designed the motor spool geometry — the spools wind the steel cables that actuate the pockets, and their geometry directly controls how the cable tension is applied during closing. I also designed the cable routing architecture and primary structural components of the gripper body.

For the pocket work, I designed and iterated three TPU pocket geometries optimized for different performance characteristics: cushioning (increased tube diameter), flexure (perforations along the contact area), and contact area (thin solid fill between tubes). Each variant was 3D printed in TPU 95A and evaluated for grip stability and surface damage.

I also ran the experimental trials — 56 tomatoes across two size groups, recording immediate damage and 5-day bruise rates. Setting up repeatable test conditions across that many samples required careful protocol discipline.

Results

Validated performance.

56 Tomatoes tested
0% Immediate damage rate
<9% Bruise rate at 5 days
9 Total components

For context: machine-based tomato harvesting typically produces 20–29% damage rates. ANGEL achieves less than half that with a simpler, lower-cost design that requires no force sensors or pneumatic actuation.
